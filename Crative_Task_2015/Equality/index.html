Gabriel Kroll

Health Care Inequality

The Swedish health care system grants those 5% of the population diagnosed with ADHD [1] access to the HandiKalender [2], a mobile application specifically tailored to their needs. But many other groups would benefit from this technology, such as those having difficulties with concentration and mental illnesses.

Creating Equality

My contribution is the generally available Drag Done app. Disorders are gradual, not black-and-white criteria by which to put people into boxes, especially when it comes to access of technological means to improve our quality of life. Therefore it is not good enough to provide some people with access to an app that implements neurofeedback and working memory training while leaving others in the cold. Technology is a powerful tool to level unequal playing fields, but in order to do so, it must be available to all, equally.

Drag Done App

Focus on Five recurring Tasks

  • create a folder that resets daily, weekly, or monthly
  • define your desired five tasks into that folder
  • assign a picture and a name for each task
  • choose a reminder alarm if you want to

Like Counting on One Hand

Drag your achieved task to the done area. You keep track of the tasks that have been accomplished. Now you stay motivated, stick to your routines and make them into neat habits. Great!

Persona

Anonymous, age: 23 years

  • doesn’t remember if something has been done already
  • forgets about daily hygiene and self-care
  • easily distracted by their surroundings
  • has no to-do app that fits their needs

It was assumed that this person has adult ADHD. But the psychiatric test showed that they couldn’t be diagnosed with this disorder, due to the lack of hyperactivity in their childhood. They get therapy for their depression and anxieties instead and will get tested for dyslexia.
